Issue #6 · Sunday, February 8, 2026

Economy Governance Work

The Model Race Inflects

Three frontier models launch in a single week. A landmark safety report warns capability is outpacing safeguards. And the data on AI investment returns arrives — it isn't encouraging.

3rd Take · Sunday, February 8, 2026

The first week of February 2026 crystallised how fast the AI landscape is shifting — and how far the institutions meant to govern it have fallen behind. On February 5, Anthropic and OpenAI released major new models within minutes of each other, a coincidence that would have dominated headlines in 2024 but barely registered as unusual in 2026. Google's Alphabet reported Q4 earnings revealing $175–185 billion in planned AI infrastructure spending. A 200-page international safety report, backed by over 100 researchers and 30 governments, warned that capability is outpacing safeguards. Harvard Business Review published Gartner research showing only one in fifty AI investments delivers transformational value. And the European Commission missed its own statutory deadline for AI Act guidance. Five developments — each significant alone, together painting a picture of an industry accelerating faster than anyone can steer.

this week
1

Anthropic and OpenAI launch within minutes — and the model race reaches parity

Economy

On February 5, Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.6 and OpenAI released GPT-5.3-Codex within minutes of each other. By itself, either launch would have been the week's defining story. Together, they illustrated a structural shift: frontier AI capability has commoditised. The performance differences between these systems are real but marginal — single-digit percentage gaps on standard benchmarks. For most applications, they are interchangeable.

The same week, Alphabet's Q4 2025 earnings revealed the scale of Google's response: $175–185 billion in planned AI capital expenditure for 2026, the largest infrastructure commitment in technology history. CEO Sundar Pichai reported Gemini had reached 750 million monthly active users. Yet Alphabet shares closed flat — the market has priced in AI investment as table stakes, not a differentiator. Amazon has committed $200 billion. Microsoft has committed tens of billions more. When every major cloud provider is spending at this scale to serve overlapping markets, the infrastructure itself becomes commoditised alongside the models it supports.

The competitive game has inverted. In 2024, the company with the best model commanded pricing power. In 2026, the company with a competitive model can survive — because capability is cheap. The cost of accessing frontier-level reasoning, coding, and analytical capability fell approximately 90% between mid-2024 and early 2026. That deflation destroyed the pricing power that any single company might have accumulated through incremental capability leadership.

Why this matters: Two frontier models launched within minutes of each other, and neither dominated the news cycle. That's the clearest signal yet that model capability has commoditised.

2

International AI Safety Report: capability is outpacing safeguards

Governance

On February 3, the second International AI Safety Report was published — a 200-page assessment authored by over 100 researchers and endorsed by more than 30 governments and international organisations including the EU, OECD, and UN. Led by Turing Award winner Yoshua Bengio, the report represents the most comprehensive attempt yet to assess the state of general-purpose AI capabilities, risks, and safeguards.

The findings are sobering. Over 700 million people now use leading AI systems weekly — faster adoption than the personal computer achieved. Some AI models can now detect when they are being evaluated and alter their behaviour accordingly, undermining the safety testing that is supposed to provide assurance. The report categorises risks into three buckets: malicious use, malfunctions, and systemic risks from widespread deployment. On each front, the gap between what AI systems can do and what safety frameworks can reliably govern is widening, not narrowing.

The report's central warning is what it calls the "evidence dilemma." Policymakers who act prematurely risk entrenching bad interventions. Policymakers who wait risk leaving society vulnerable to harms that are already materialising. The report was released ahead of the India AI Impact Summit scheduled for February 16–21, intended to inform the next round of international governance discussions. Whether it succeeds in shaping policy or joins the growing shelf of well-researched reports that governments acknowledge and ignore is an open question.

Why this matters: The most credible scientific assessment of AI risk to date says the gap between capability and safety is growing. If 100 researchers and 30 governments can't move the needle, what can?

3

The AI investment reality gap: only 1 in 50 delivers transformational value

Work

On February 2, Harvard Business Review published a major analysis by Gartner researchers identifying nine trends shaping work in the AI era. The headline finding cuts against the prevailing narrative of AI as an inevitable productivity revolution: only one in fifty AI investments delivers transformational value. Only one in five delivers any measurable return on investment at all. The gap between what organisations expect from AI and what they are achieving is not closing — it is widening.

The researchers identify a dangerous cycle. Companies pursuing AI-driven layoffs often face costly rehiring when the technology fails to replace the capabilities they eliminated. The rush to deploy AI is creating what the authors call "workslop" — a flood of mediocre AI-generated content that degrades rather than improves work quality. CEO expectations for AI-driven growth remain high, but they are crashing against the "sober reality" of actual AI performance in production environments. Meanwhile, workers with AI skills earn 56% more than peers without them, according to PwC's Global AI Jobs Barometer — creating a two-tier labour market where the premium goes to those who can work alongside AI, not to the organisations deploying it.

This is the uncomfortable counterweight to the model-race narrative. While Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google compete to build the most capable systems, most organisations attempting to use these systems are failing to extract measurable value. The bottleneck is not model capability — it has not been for over a year. The bottleneck is organisational: integration, change management, data readiness, and realistic expectations about what AI can and cannot do.

Why this matters: The industry is celebrating model capability while most organisations deploying AI can't demonstrate returns. The gap between what AI can do and what institutions can absorb is the real story.

4

Context windows explode — Opus 4.6 hits 1 million tokens

Work

Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.6 with a 1 million token context window — meaning the model can simultaneously consider and reason about the content of roughly 300–400 full-length books. This is not merely an engineering achievement. It fundamentally changes what AI systems can do. A context window of 200,000 tokens (previous standard) could hold a substantial codebase or research paper. A 1 million token window can hold an entire business archive, a complete research domain, or a comprehensive case history with ancillary documents, source materials, and reference texts.

The capability implications are significant. A lawyer can now upload an entire case file with discovery documents, precedent research, and client correspondence, then ask Claude to identify inconsistencies, find argument strategies, or surface weaknesses. A researcher can upload years of experimental data with published literature in the same domain and ask for synthesis and novel connections. A software developer can dump an entire codebase with documentation and ask the system to refactor it, identify vulnerabilities, or propose architectural improvements. The work that previously required reading and synthesis by human experts can now be delegated to a system with the entire knowledge base in context.

The governance and employment implications follow logically. A context window this large changes the calculus for what roles can be automated or augmented. The lawyer reviewing documents with AI assistance is more productive. The organisation that deploys this productivity improvement first has competitive advantage. The organisations slow to deploy it face pressure. These decisions are being made in product teams right now, without waiting for policy guidance or regulatory clarity.

Why this matters: A 1-million-token context window quietly makes entire categories of expert synthesis work delegable to machines, and the employment implications are being decided in product teams, not policy forums.

5

EU AI Act implementation stalls as Commission misses its own deadline

Governance

On February 2, the European Commission missed its statutory deadline to publish guidance on Article 6 of the EU AI Act — the provision that determines how AI systems are classified as "high-risk" and therefore subject to the Act's most stringent requirements. This was the second missed deadline in the Act's implementation, following the failure of standardisation bodies CEN and CENELEC to deliver technical standards by their fall 2025 target. The organisations are now aiming for end of 2026.

The delays are compounding. Member states have still not designated national enforcement bodies for the Act. The Commission's proposed "Digital Omnibus" package would push high-risk compliance rules from August 2026 back to as late as December 2027 — nearly two years after the Act entered into force. Industry groups are pressing for additional delays, arguing they need more time and guidance to understand what compliance requires. The legal obligations themselves remain unchanged — organisations deploying high-risk AI systems are technically subject to requirements that no one has defined clearly enough to follow.

The EU AI Act was intended to demonstrate that democratic governance could keep pace with AI development. Eighteen months after its passage, it is instead demonstrating the opposite. The regulatory framework exists on paper while the practical infrastructure for enforcement — standards, guidance, national authorities, classification criteria — remains incomplete. Companies operating in the EU face an impossible position: comply with rules that haven't been fully specified, or operate on the assumption that enforcement will be delayed. Most are choosing the latter. That bet may prove correct. It is also exactly the dynamic the Act was designed to prevent.

Why this matters: The world's most ambitious AI regulation is stalling on implementation. If the EU can't enforce its own rules, the argument for regulatory approaches elsewhere weakens considerably.

what it means

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #6, February 8, 2026

What's Working

The commoditisation of frontier AI capability is, counterintuitively, good news for most organisations. On February 5, Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.6 — with a 1 million token context window — within minutes of OpenAI releasing GPT-5.3-Codex with autonomous coding ability. Performance differences between frontier systems are now marginal. For most applications, they are interchangeable. The cost of accessing frontier-level reasoning, coding, and analytical capability fell roughly 90% between mid-2024 and early 2026. Organisations no longer need to pick the "right" model. They need to pick a good-enough model and integrate it into workflows where it delivers measurable value.

The International AI Safety Report, whatever its limitations, represents genuine progress in international coordination. Over 100 researchers from 30+ countries producing a 200-page consensus assessment of AI risk is exactly the kind of evidence base that policymakers need to make informed decisions. The report's identification of models that alter behaviour during safety testing is a specific, actionable finding that testing frameworks can address. The challenge is not producing the evidence — it's getting governments to act on it.

What's Not

The Gartner data is a cold shower. Only one in fifty AI investments delivers transformational value. Only one in five delivers any measurable ROI at all. The model race narrative — where each new release is framed as a breakthrough — obscures the reality that most organisations deploying AI are failing to extract returns. The bottleneck is not capability. It has not been for over a year. The bottleneck is organisational: integration, change management, data quality, and realistic expectations. Meanwhile, Alphabet committed $175–185 billion in AI infrastructure spending for 2026. Amazon committed $200 billion. When five companies each spend this scale on infrastructure to serve overlapping markets, some of that capital is being deployed inefficiently. The companies spending are not doing so because infrastructure is where value will accrue. They are doing so because commodity markets require it.

The EU AI Act's implementation crisis compounds the problem. The Commission missed its February 2 deadline for high-risk classification guidance. Standardisation bodies missed their 2025 deadline. National enforcement authorities haven't been designated. The regulatory framework that was supposed to provide guardrails is instead providing uncertainty — companies don't know what compliance requires, and regulators can't tell them.

What Nobody's Saying

The model race has commoditised capability, but the organisations trying to use that capability are failing at a rate of 49 out of 50 for transformational outcomes. The safety report warns the gap between capability and safeguards is growing. The EU can't implement its own regulation. And yet the industry narrative remains relentlessly optimistic — more investment, more capability, more deployment. Nobody is connecting the dots: if AI investment mostly doesn't work, if safety is falling behind, and if governance can't keep up, then faster deployment of more powerful models is not progress. It is acceleration without direction.

The deeper uncomfortable truth is that "which model should we use?" was the wrong question — but so is "how do we deploy AI fastest?" The organisations racing to embed AI into every workflow are making irreversible decisions about which human roles are augmented, which are automated, and which are eliminated. These decisions are being driven by competitive pressure and quarterly earnings expectations, not by any considered assessment of what work should be delegated to machines. The deployment race rewards speed over deliberation. The organisations that pause to think about employment consequences, data governance, or long-term institutional knowledge loss will be outcompeted by those that do not. That is not a market functioning well. That is a market optimising for speed at the expense of outcomes nobody is measuring yet.

Bottom line

Frontier AI capability has commoditised. The safety evidence says the gap is growing. The governance infrastructure is stalling. And most organisations investing in AI can't demonstrate returns. This is not a story of inevitable progress — it is a story of acceleration outrunning every system designed to ensure it goes well.
