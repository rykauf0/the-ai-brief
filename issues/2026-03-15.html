The Bill Comes Due

Five developments — and one uncomfortable question about what happens when the commitments are irreversible but the evidence isn’t in.

3rd Take · Sunday, March 15, 2026

This was the week that institutions stopped hedging and started committing — to AI deployments, to legal battles over AI’s boundaries, to infrastructure bets that reshape how cities get electricity. The Pentagon blacklisted an American AI company for having ethical limits. A federal judge drew the first legal line around what AI agents can do on the open internet. Companies poured $667 billion into AI infrastructure while their own data showed the productivity gains aren’t materialising. AI-generated deepfakes invaded both the midterm elections and the Iran war at a scale that overwhelmed fact-checkers. And the UK government proposed letting data centres jump the electricity grid queue ahead of new homes. Five developments. Five irreversible consequences. One common thread: the evidence that any of it is working hasn’t caught up to the damage being done.

this week
1

The Pentagon blacklists Anthropic — for having ethical limits

Governance

On March 9, Anthropic filed two federal lawsuits against the Trump administration after the Pentagon designated the AI company a “supply chain risk” — a label historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei and Kaspersky. The designation followed weeks of failed negotiations over two red lines Anthropic refused to cross: allowing its technology to be used for mass surveillance of American citizens, and powering fully autonomous weapons without human oversight. Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth argued the Pentagon should have access to AI systems for “any lawful purpose” and that no private contractor should dictate terms. The February 27 order required federal agencies and military contractors to halt business with Anthropic.

The lawsuits, filed in California and the D.C. Circuit, allege First Amendment violations and an unlawful expansion of supply-chain-risk authority beyond what Congress intended. Anthropic’s CFO estimated the designation could reduce 2026 revenue by “multiple billions of dollars,” with more than 100 enterprise customers raising concerns about continued use of the company’s products. In an unusual move, dozens of scientists and researchers at OpenAI and Google DeepMind filed an amicus brief supporting Anthropic, arguing the designation harms U.S. competitiveness and chills public discussion about AI risks. A hearing has been moved up to March 24.

The timing is instructive. OpenAI struck a deal with the Pentagon just hours after the administration’s order against Anthropic. The market is reading the signal clearly: the competitive advantage now goes to companies willing to operate without ethical restrictions on military use.

Why this matters: For the first time, the U.S. government has used national security law — designed to counter foreign adversaries — against a domestic company for maintaining safety limits on its own technology. The precedent extends far beyond one company.

2

A federal judge draws the first legal line on AI agents

Governance Economy

On March 9, U.S. District Judge Maxine Chesney issued a preliminary injunction barring Perplexity’s Comet browser from accessing Amazon user accounts or making purchases on their behalf — the first major court ruling on autonomous AI shopping agents. The legal finding is precise and consequential: Amazon provided “strong evidence” that Perplexity violated the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act because Comet accessed the platform “with the Amazon user’s permission, but without authorization by Amazon.” Citing Ninth Circuit precedent, including Facebook, Inc. v. Power Ventures, Inc., the court held that once a platform revokes authorization, continued access may violate the CFAA even if users themselves consent.

The backstory adds weight. Amazon warned Perplexity at least five times starting in November 2024. In August 2025, Amazon implemented a technical barrier to block Comet’s access. Within 24 hours, Perplexity released an update disguising its agent as a Chrome browser to circumvent the block — giving Amazon exactly the intentionality argument the court found persuasive. Perplexity has appealed to the Ninth Circuit. The injunction requires Perplexity to destroy all Amazon data collected through Comet sessions.

Why this matters: This ruling separates user consent from platform consent — a distinction that will define what AI agents can and cannot do across the entire internet, not just on Amazon.

3

$667 billion in AI spending. No macro productivity gains. More work for everyone.

Economy Work

Three separate data points converged this week to paint a picture that challenges the central promise of AI investment. Fortune reported that companies using AI are not sending workers home early — they are demanding more output from the same employees. Mike Manos, CTO at Dun & Bradstreet, told Fortune he “got the eight hours to two hours” but now gets 20 hours of work done per employee instead. Workers using AI tools are spending up to 346% more time on tasks, with data described as “unambiguous: AI does not reduce workloads.” Separately, KPMG’s 2026 U.S. CEO Outlook Pulse Survey found that 55% of CEOs expect AI to increase hiring — not reduce it — and only 9% plan workforce reductions. Yet 77% of those same CEOs agreed that generative AI was overhyped in the past year.

The macro picture is starker. Goldman Sachs’ analysis of fourth-quarter earnings found “no meaningful relationship between productivity and AI adoption at the economy-wide level,” even as analysts revised 2026 hyperscaler capex expectations to $667 billion — a 62% jump from 2025. Only 10% of S&P 500 management teams quantified AI’s impact on specific use cases. Goldman projects the net GDP impact of all this spending at 0.1 to 0.2 percentage points. The gap between investment and return has never been wider.

Why this matters: The productivity paradox is no longer theoretical. Companies are spending hundreds of billions on AI, and the measurable result so far is that employees are doing more work, not less.

4

AI deepfakes cross the line — in elections and in war

Society

Two developments this week demonstrated that AI-generated misinformation has crossed a critical threshold. On March 13, Senate Republicans released an online ad in which a realistic but entirely fabricated version of Democratic Texas Senate candidate James Talarico speaks directly into the camera for over a minute — the first political deepfake of a candidate talking at length in a lifelike manner. UC Berkeley digital forensics professor Hany Farid called the face and voice “very good,” noting only a “slight misalignment between audio and video.” The words “AI GENERATED” appear in small text for roughly three seconds, then shrink to near-invisibility for the remainder. Texas law criminalises deceptive deepfakes only within 30 days of an election — a window this ad fell outside.

Simultaneously, the Iran conflict has unleashed what researchers describe as the largest flood of AI-generated war imagery ever seen. AI-created videos on X depict American soldiers captured by Iran, Israeli cities in ruins, and U.S. embassies ablaze — racking up tens of millions of views before fact-checkers can respond. In one case, X’s own chatbot Grok described an AI-generated image as “a real photo from today’s Iranian ballistic missile strikes,” incorrectly citing Reuters and CNN as sources. X responded by suspending creators from its revenue-sharing programme for 90 days if they post AI-generated conflict videos without disclosure, but the policy relies on self-reporting in an environment where deception is the point.

Why this matters: AI-generated misinformation is no longer a hypothetical concern. It is now being deployed at scale in both democratic elections and active warfare, and the gap between fabrication speed and verification capacity is widening, not narrowing.

5

UK data centres jump the grid queue — at housing’s expense

Environment Society

On March 11, the UK government launched a consultation on electricity grid reforms that would prioritise AI data centres and industrial sites over other connections — including housing. The numbers driving the decision are staggering: approximately 140 data centre projects are seeking grid connections with a combined demand of around 50 gigawatts, exceeding Britain’s current peak electricity demand of roughly 45 GW. The grid connection queue has grown by 460% in six months. Projects now face waits of up to fifteen years to connect.

The Home Builders Federation warned that excluding housing from the priority list would amount to an “effective moratorium” on new homes in capacity-constrained areas. The concern is not hypothetical: a London Assembly report found that West London’s local grid hit full capacity in 2022, stalling planned housing developments. A single AI data centre consumes as much electricity as 100,000 homes. Data centres already receive preferential planning treatment as “critical national infrastructure” and cannot be blocked by local objections. The consultation closes April 15.

Why this matters: This is the first time a government has explicitly proposed giving AI infrastructure priority access to electricity over housing — making the resource trade-off between AI growth and human shelter a matter of official policy rather than market competition.

Sources

  • Construction Enquirer, “Data centre sites to jump queue for grid connections”
  • The Register, “AI datacenters could get priority access to Britain’s grid”
what it means

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #11, March 15, 2026

What's Working

The legal and institutional framework for AI is finally catching up to the technology. Judge Chesney’s ruling in the Perplexity case establishes a clear, workable principle — user consent does not equal platform consent — that gives the entire agentic commerce ecosystem something it badly needed: a legal boundary. Anthropic’s lawsuit, whatever its outcome, forces a public reckoning with the question of whether AI companies can maintain ethical limits in the face of government pressure, and the amicus brief from scientists at rival companies suggests the research community takes that question seriously. The UK grid consultation, for all its problems, at least makes the energy trade-off explicit rather than letting it happen by default. Naming the problem is the prerequisite to solving it.

What's Not

The productivity evidence is damning. $667 billion in 2026 hyperscaler capex, a 62% increase from last year, and Goldman Sachs finds no meaningful economy-wide productivity link. Only 10% of S&P 500 management teams can even quantify AI’s impact on their own operations. Workers are not being freed by AI — they are being loaded with more tasks. Meanwhile, AI’s most visible societal impact this week was not productivity but deception: deepfakes sophisticated enough to fool digital forensics experts deployed in both a Senate race and an active war, with platforms reduced to after-the-fact revenue-sharing suspensions as their primary countermeasure. The pattern across all five developments is the same: the commitments are enormous and the safeguards are not keeping pace.

What Nobody's Saying

The uncomfortable truth connecting these stories is that AI has become a faith-based investment with real-world casualties. The Pentagon is willing to blacklist a domestic company rather than accept limits on AI it hasn’t proven it can use effectively. Companies are spending at levels that require $600 billion in annual AI revenue to justify, according to J.P. Morgan, but can’t demonstrate productivity gains at the macro level. The UK is proposing to give AI data centres priority over homes for electricity on the strength of projected economic benefits that no current data supports. And the same technology being deployed for productivity and defence is simultaneously being used to fabricate political candidates’ words and manufacture images of wars that didn’t happen — with no institution capable of responding at the speed required. The bets are real. The returns remain theoretical. And the institutions making these bets have structured them so that reversing course will be far more expensive than pressing forward.

Bottom line

This was the week AI stopped being a technology story and became a resource-allocation story — of money, of legal precedent, of physical infrastructure, and of public trust. Governments, corporations, and courts are all making irreversible commitments on the basis of AI’s projected benefits while its demonstrated harms, from fabricated political speech to manufactured war imagery, outpace every safeguard. The organisations that will navigate this best are not the ones betting biggest. They are the ones building in the ability to change course when the evidence arrives.
