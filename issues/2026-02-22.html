Issue #8 · Sunday, February 22, 2026

Environment Economy

The Environmental Reckoning

The physical infrastructure of artificial intelligence is now vast enough to reshape energy policy and resource allocation across entire regions.

The AI Brief · Sunday, February 22, 2026

Every query answered and every model trained consumes electricity and water — at a scale that is now impossible to ignore. The AI boom of 2024–2026 has been accompanied by a quiet but equally consequential story: the physical infrastructure of intelligence is vast, resource-intensive, and reshaping energy and environmental policy in real time. This week's briefing examines what the environmental costs of AI actually are, and what the honest accounting requires.

this week
1

Global data centre electricity consumption will exceed 1,000 terawatt-hours by 2026 — roughly Japan's entire national consumption

Environment & Planet

The International Energy Agency's 2025 electricity report projects global data centre power consumption doubling from roughly 500 TWh in 2022 to 1,000 TWh by 2026. AI workloads, which were negligible in 2022, now account for approximately half of that consumption. Goldman Sachs projects AI will require 47 additional gigawatts of US power capacity by 2030 — equivalent to the entire power generation capacity of France. The scale is no longer a fringe concern. Data centre electricity consumption is now a primary driver of broader electricity demand growth, with accelerated server electricity consumption growing at 30% annually compared to 9% for conventional servers.

The honest accounting creates a tension that organizations with sustainability commitments cannot easily resolve. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have all publicly committed to net-zero operations, yet their actual carbon emissions have increased as data centre construction has accelerated. Microsoft's emissions rose approximately 30% between 2020 and 2024, even as the company expanded renewable energy procurement. The issue is not renewable energy availability — it is the pace of new construction outstripping the pace of renewable energy procurement. For any organization simultaneously adopting AI at scale and maintaining climate commitments, the math is unresolved: you cannot have unlimited data centre expansion and stable carbon emissions unless you have renewable energy generation that grows faster than the data centres consume it.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth — every major tech company has publicly committed to net-zero while their actual emissions climb, and no one has reconciled the math between unlimited AI expansion and stable carbon footprints.

2

Data centre water consumption is creating regional resource crises

Environment & Planet

Data centres consumed approximately 17 billion gallons of water in 2023, primarily for cooling systems. Projections show water usage surging to 68 billion gallons by 2028 — a 300% increase in five years. A single hyperscale data centre can use as much water daily as a small city. The geographic concentration creates local crises: the five states with the most data centre infrastructure — California, Arizona, Texas, Illinois, and Virginia — include some of the most water-stressed regions in the United States. Arizona, experiencing severe drought conditions due to Colorado River shortage and depleting groundwater, has implemented water use restrictions while simultaneously approving major new data centre construction from Google, Microsoft, and Meta.

Water scarcity is a local problem with no global substitute. Unlike carbon emissions, which diffuse globally, water consumption affects specific communities directly. The infrastructure costs are staggering: without new water efficiencies, data centre cooling systems could require 697 million to 1.45 billion gallons of additional peak water capacity per day by 2030 — equivalent to New York City's daily water supply — with infrastructure costs estimated at $10–58 billion. State legislatures and water authorities in affected regions are beginning to push back. The European Commission expects to roll out regulation requiring data centre operators to set minimum water efficiency standards. Several US states are considering similar restrictions. The question is no longer abstract: who pays for the water infrastructure expansion required to cool the data centres, and what happens when local water resources cannot expand fast enough?

Why this matters: This is a setback with no easy fix — unlike carbon, water is a local resource with no global market, and data centres are being built in drought-stricken regions that are already rationing supply for residents and farms.

3

Nuclear power is experiencing a renaissance — driven entirely by AI data centre demand

Environment & Planet

Three Mile Island, site of America's worst nuclear accident, is being restarted to power Microsoft's AI data centres. The facility, shut down in 2019, will provide 835 megawatts of carbon-free electricity under a historic 20-year power purchase agreement with Microsoft. Google announced deals with Kairos Power to purchase electricity from small modular reactors beginning in 2030. Amazon has invested in multiple nuclear startups. The driver is consistent across all: AI data centres require firm, dispatchable power — electricity available on demand, 24/7 — rather than intermittent renewable power. Wind and solar produce electricity when conditions allow; nuclear produces electricity when AI needs it.

The nuclear revival driven by AI demand is one of the more unexpected geopolitical and energy policy stories of early 2026. Countries with strong nuclear industries — France, South Korea, the United States — have structural energy advantages for the AI era that countries that phased out nuclear do not. Germany, which shut down its last nuclear plant in 2023, is now importing nuclear power from France to meet industrial electricity demand. The energy policy decisions of the past decade, made without reference to AI, are now shaping AI competitiveness in ways policymakers never anticipated. This is not a pure environmental win — nuclear carries genuine safety risks and unresolved waste disposal challenges — but it is a signal that the energy infrastructure required for AI is reshaping geopolitical competition in real time.

Why this matters: This is genuine progress with a large asterisk — AI demand is making carbon-free nuclear power commercially viable again, but the revival comes with unresolved safety and waste challenges that got nuclear shut down in the first place.

4

Hardware refresh cycles are generating e-waste faster than recycling infrastructure can handle

Environment & Planet

The extraordinary pace of AI chip improvement — Nvidia's Hopper H100 GPUs released in 2022 are already being replaced in major data centres by newer architectures — is creating premature hardware obsolescence at scale. Refresh cycles have compressed to 18–36 months. Data centres that invested heavily in H100 infrastructure in 2023–2024 are facing difficult choices: retain hardware that is now two generations behind, or replace it and generate significant e-waste disposal challenges. The global e-waste stream was already approximately 62 million tonnes per year before the AI buildout accelerated hardware replacement cycles. Projections suggest AI could add 1.2 to 5 million metric tonnes of additional e-waste annually by 2030 — representing up to 12% of global e-waste.

E-waste governance is one of the least-addressed environmental dimensions of the AI transition. Rare earth metals in AI chips — neodymium, dysprosium, gallium, germanium — require energy-intensive extraction and processing; their disposal creates toxic contamination risk. Less than 25% of global e-waste is properly recycled, and advanced AI hardware is materially complex and designed for compactness rather than disassembly. The supply chain for rare earth metals runs predominantly through China, which has imposed periodic export restrictions. The combination of accelerating hardware replacement cycles, inadequate e-waste infrastructure, and supply chain concentration is creating an environmental liability that is not currently being priced into AI's cost accounting.

Why this matters: This is the environmental cost nobody is pricing in — 18-month hardware refresh cycles generate millions of tonnes of toxic waste, and the economics of recycling rare earth metals still cannot compete with virgin extraction.

what it means

What It All Means

The AI Brief · Issue #8, February 22, 2026

The environmental costs of AI have moved from theoretical concern to material reality. Data centres now consume approximately 1–2% of global electricity, with growth rates four times faster than electricity consumption generally. Water stress is acute in affected regions and worsening. Hardware obsolescence is generating e-waste faster than infrastructure can handle. These are not speculative future harms — they are present, documented, measurable, and growing. The question is no longer whether environmental costs exist; it is how they will be accounted for and who will bear them.

The nuclear story is the most strategically consequential because it reveals how AI is reshaping energy geopolitics. The technology that would have remained niche without climate pressure — small modular reactors, modern nuclear facilities — has suddenly become commercially viable because AI data centres will pay premium prices for firm power. Countries that retained nuclear capacity have an advantage that countries that phased it out do not. This was not part of any deliberate geopolitical strategy; it is an unintended consequence of AI's energy hunger meeting the grid's capacity constraints.

The water crisis story is perhaps the most immediate and hardest to solve. Carbon emissions are global problems that can be addressed with global energy transitions. Water is a local resource with no substitute and no global market. A data centre going into an already-stressed water region is competing directly with agriculture, municipalities, and ecosystems for a finite resource. The European Commission recognizing this and moving toward water efficiency regulation is appropriate; US state-level pushback is inevitable and probably necessary. The question that state legislators and water authorities are asking — who pays for the infrastructure required to cool these data centres — is not rhetorical. It is the question that will determine whether the current data centre expansion trajectories are sustainable.

The e-waste story is the most structurally difficult because it involves global supply chains, Chinese export controls, and the economics of hardware recycling. Less than 25% of global e-waste is properly recycled because recycling rare earth metals from complex electronics is expensive and technically difficult. As long as virgin rare earth extraction is cheaper than recycling, companies will extract rather than recycle. The scale of AI e-waste is large enough that this may change, but the change requires either regulatory mandates or economic incentives that don't currently exist.

The honest accounting that organizations with sustainability commitments need to make is straightforward: what is the actual energy intensity of your AI deployment, what is the actual carbon footprint of that deployment given your energy mix, what is your actual water footprint in affected regions, and what is your plan for hardware end-of-life? Organizations that can answer those questions have a governance structure. Organizations that cannot are making implicit bets about how long they can operate without accountability, and those bets are getting shorter as regulators move faster.

Bottom line

AI's environmental footprint is now large enough to be a primary driver of energy policy, water allocation, and geopolitical competition. Organizations and governments that treat this as a governance challenge will be better positioned than those that treat it as a marketing message or someone else's problem.
