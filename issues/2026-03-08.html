The Jobs Question Gets Real

Five developments — and why they need to be read together.

3rd Take · Sunday, March 8, 2026

The week that produced the most rigorous empirical study of AI's labour market effects to date also produced a voluntary energy pledge with no enforcement mechanism, the first serious signs of AI job loss entering electoral politics, a CEO publicly predicting the elimination of half of all entry-level white-collar jobs while his company's research arm published evidence suggesting those fears were premature — and two major companies cutting tens of thousands of jobs in the name of AI while posting record profits. Five distinct stories. One coherent signal.

this week
1

The most rigorous AI labour study yet — and what it actually shows

Work

Anthropic economists Maxim Massenkoff and Peter McCrory published "Labor market impacts of AI: A new measure and early evidence" — the first major study to use actual AI usage data rather than theoretical capability estimates. Instead of asking "what tasks could AI theoretically do?", they measured what AI is actually being used for, tracking real usage patterns from millions of Claude conversations against the US Department of Labor's O*NET occupational database. The resulting "observed exposure" metric is more grounded than any prior measure.

The headline finding: no systematic increase in unemployment for highly AI-exposed workers since late 2022. For workers in the most exposed occupations, the jobs picture has not materially worsened. The finding that most headlines buried: a statistically significant 14–16% decline in job-finding rates for workers aged 22–25 in AI-exposed occupations since ChatGPT's introduction. No mass unemployment — but a measurable contraction at the point where the next generation of professionals enters the labour market. The gap between theory and practice is also striking: in computer and mathematical occupations, AI could theoretically handle 94% of tasks; actual measured usage covers roughly 33%.

The study's "so far" caveat is not a throwaway qualifier. It is the most important phrase in the document. The forces at work are not spent. The 33% actual coverage expanding toward 94% theoretical capability is not an event — it is a process already underway.

Why this matters: This is genuine progress — the first empirical study that measures what AI is actually doing to jobs rather than what it theoretically could, and the gap between the two is the most important finding.

2

Big Tech signs the "bring your own power" pledge — and the fine print matters

Environment

On March 4, President Trump formalized the Ratepayer Protection Pledge at the White House. Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI signed a commitment to build, buy, or bring their own electricity generation for new data centers — covering the full cost of power delivery infrastructure rather than passing those costs to household utility ratepayers. The announcement was timed to electoral politics: rising electricity bills tied to data centre expansion have become a genuine voter grievance, and the pledge gave both the administration and tech companies a response.

The enforcement problem is significant. The pledge is voluntary. The White House has the bully pulpit but no legal authority to impose rate structures or require power purchase agreements. Ari Peskoe of Harvard's Electricity Law Initiative told CNN directly: the commitment "doesn't have teeth." What happens when a company's preferred power source isn't available, or when the economics of self-supply shift? The answer isn't in the pledge document, because there's no mechanism to compel an answer. The pledge also arrives against a backdrop in which the same companies are still negotiating for grid access to power their existing facilities — the new-construction commitment doesn't resolve the existing grid impact question.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth nobody wants to name — a voluntary, unenforceable pledge is being treated as a policy solution to a structural infrastructure problem that requires regulation.

3

AI job loss goes electoral

Governance

A new NBC News report documented a shift that was coming: politicians across the ideological spectrum are beginning to treat white-collar AI job displacement as an electoral issue. Senators Josh Hawley and Bernie Sanders — as different as two legislators get — have both sounded alarms. Florida Governor Ron DeSantis has repeatedly warned that white-collar occupations "could end up being obsolete." California Governor Gavin Newsom has made AI worker protection part of his economic agenda. A majority of voters now tell NBC pollsters that the risks of AI outweigh the benefits. Two new midterm-focused Super PACs have formed on either side: the pro-innovation "Leading the Future" (backed by $125 million, including OpenAI president Greg Brockman) and the regulatory advocacy group Public First ($50 million, including a $20 million contribution from Anthropic).

The political timing matters. The 2026 midterms will be the first federal election cycle in which AI job displacement is an explicit campaign issue rather than a background concern. The constituency taking shape is unusual: it includes white-collar professionals in previously secure roles — lawyers, analysts, programmers, accountants — who have historically not identified with labour movements or economic anxiety politics. If that constituency mobilises, the political coalitions that have shaped tech policy for a decade will face pressure they haven't encountered before.

Why this matters: This is genuine progress — AI displacement is finally entering democratic accountability, but the political system is only now building the vocabulary for a problem that's been developing for three years.

4

The CEO warning and the data: a company at odds with itself

Work

The same week Anthropic published research finding limited current labour market impact, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei gave public interviews warning that AI could eliminate 50% of all entry-level white-collar jobs within five years — with US unemployment potentially reaching 10–20% in extreme scenarios. He is not alone: executives at Ford, Amazon, Salesforce, and JPMorgan Chase have made comparable predictions. A Harvard Business Review analysis published in January found that companies are already laying off workers not because AI has demonstrably replaced their output, but because of AI's expected future performance — a distinction with serious implications for how we interpret employment data right now.

The internal tension at Anthropic is not a contradiction to dismiss — it is analytically important. The research team is measuring what AI usage is doing to the labour market today. The CEO is communicating what he believes AI will be capable of doing within a few years. Both can be true simultaneously: limited current impact and serious near-term risk. But they produce very different responses from employers, policymakers, and workers — and the CEO's public statements shape behaviour in ways that the research team's careful findings do not. When major employers hear a frontier lab CEO predict 50% entry-level elimination, some of them begin making hiring decisions accordingly, before the data would justify it. The expectation becomes partially self-fulfilling.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth — CEO predictions of mass displacement are causing preemptive layoffs before the data justifies them, making the forecasts self-fulfilling in ways no study is designed to measure.

5

The layoffs that make the theory concrete

Economy

While Anthropic published research and its CEO made predictions, two companies acted. Oracle disclosed plans to cut 20,000–30,000 employees — up to 18% of its 162,000-person workforce — to free $8–10 billion in cash flow for AI data centre construction. This is not a company in trouble: remaining performance obligations stood at $523 billion, up 433% year over year. The cuts are a direct trade — people for infrastructure — to fund a $50 billion capital expenditure commitment including a $156 billion deal to provide compute for OpenAI. Returns on that spending are not expected until approximately 2030.

Days earlier, Block CEO Jack Dorsey cut more than 4,000 employees — nearly half the company's workforce — explicitly citing AI as the reason. Block reported $2.87 billion in gross profit last quarter, up 24% year over year. Dorsey wrote that "the intelligence tools we're creating and using, paired with smaller and flatter teams, are enabling a new way of working," and predicted that "within the next year, the majority of companies will reach the same conclusion." Block's shares rose 24% on the announcement. An Oxford Economics report from January complicates the narrative: many layoffs CEOs attribute to AI are actually corrections for pandemic-era overhiring. But even if the AI rationale is partly cover, the market's enthusiastic response to headcount reduction creates its own incentive structure.

Why this matters: This is what the theory looks like in practice — profitable companies cutting tens of thousands of jobs not because AI has replaced the work, but because markets reward the promise that it will.

what it means

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #10, March 8, 2026

What's Working

Anthropic's "observed exposure" metric is a genuine methodological advance — the first measure of what AI is actually doing to jobs rather than what it theoretically could. The 33% actual vs. 94% theoretical capability gap means the transition is still early enough for institutions to adapt. And AI displacement is finally entering the democratic process: bipartisan political attention, midterm Super PACs forming on both sides, and a majority of voters telling pollsters the risks outweigh the benefits. The feedback loop between economic disruption and democratic accountability is beginning to function.

What's Not

The 14–16% decline in job-finding rates for workers aged 22–25 in AI-exposed occupations is the most important finding and received the least attention — these are statistics about blocked entry, not displacement. No current policy framework addresses this. Meanwhile, the Ratepayer Protection Pledge is a voluntary commitment with no enforcement mechanism being treated as a policy solution to a structural infrastructure problem. And Oracle and Block are cutting tens of thousands of jobs from positions of financial strength — markets rewarding headcount reduction regardless of whether AI has actually replaced the work.

What Nobody's Saying

CEO predictions of mass displacement are causing the displacement they predict. Companies are making layoff decisions based on AI's expected performance rather than its demonstrated performance — and markets are rewarding them for it, creating a self-reinforcing cycle no study is designed to measure. The 33% actual usage against 94% theoretical capability is not a reassuring gap; it is a measure of how much displacement is still in the pipeline. The "so far" caveat in the Anthropic study is the most important phrase in the document.

Bottom line

The jobs question is no longer abstract or academic. It's in the data, in the CEO forecasts, in the polls, and now in the campaign ads. The organisations best positioned for what comes next are not the ones waiting for clarity — they are the ones designing professional development pathways that don't depend on the bottom rungs of the career ladder still existing. That window is narrowing.
