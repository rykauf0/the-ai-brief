The Preemption Doctrine

Five developments — and why they need to be read together.

3rd Take · Sunday, March 22, 2026

A week in which the federal government moved to preempt state AI regulation, a single developer’s weekend project demonstrated AI agents conducting autonomous scientific research, the world’s most valuable chipmaker projected a trillion dollars in hardware orders, a global trust survey showed Americans among the most sceptical populations on earth about the technology their own companies lead — and the electricity bills arrived. Five developments. One pattern: the infrastructure is accelerating faster than the institutions meant to govern it.

this week
1

The White House moves to preempt state AI laws

Governance

On March 20, the Trump administration released its National AI Legislative Framework — a blueprint urging Congress to establish federal AI rules that would override the patchwork of over 1,000 state-level AI bills introduced since 2023. The framework’s seven pillars cover child safety, intellectual property, free speech, workforce development, and energy permitting. The centrepiece is preemption: the White House wants Congress to strip states of the authority to regulate how AI models are developed, arguing that conflicting rules across fifty jurisdictions would “undermine American innovation and the ability to lead in the global AI race.”

The framework is also notable for what it does not propose. There would be no new federal AI regulator. Instead, existing sector-specific agencies — the FDA for health, the SEC for finance — would absorb AI oversight into their existing mandates. Liability protections for AI developers are explicitly recommended, with the White House warning against “open-ended liability” that “could give rise to excessive litigation.” On copyright, the administration punts: whether training AI on copyrighted content constitutes fair use is left to the courts. The administration says it wants legislation “this year,” but with razor-thin congressional margins and midterms in November, the framework may end up being more significant as a statement of principle than a legislative roadmap.

Why this matters: The federal government has chosen its lane — innovation-first, liability-light, and hostile to state-level experimentation. Whether that’s a coherent strategy or a regulatory vacuum dressed in policy language will depend entirely on whether Congress acts before the states do.

2

Karpathy’s autoresearch: AI agents doing science overnight

Work

Andrej Karpathy — former Tesla AI director and OpenAI co-founder — released autoresearch, a 630-line open-source script that lets an AI agent autonomously modify machine learning training code, run five-minute experiments, evaluate results, and iterate without human oversight. Left running for two days on a single GPU, the agent completed roughly 700 experiments and found 20 additive improvements that transferred to larger models, reducing the time to reach GPT-2–level performance by 11%. The repository hit 49,000 GitHub stars within two weeks. Shopify CEO Tobias Lütke pointed autoresearch at an internal model and reported a 19% improvement after 37 overnight experiments.

The same week, Karpathy released a second tool: an interactive visualisation mapping AI exposure across 342 US occupations using Bureau of Labor Statistics data. An LLM scored each job on a 0–10 exposure scale. The finding that went viral: professions earning over $100,000 averaged 6.7 exposure, while those under $35,000 averaged 3.4. Software developers scored 8–9. Plumbers scored near zero. Karpathy took the chart down after widespread misinterpretation, noting that a high score does not predict job elimination — software demand could grow as each developer becomes more productive. But the damage framing was already in the news cycle.

Why this matters: Autoresearch is not self-improving AI — it’s an agent optimising a smaller, separate model. But it is a concrete demonstration that AI can now conduct the iterative hypothesis-test-learn cycle that constitutes a significant portion of research work. The jobs exposure tool, however imperfect, put a number next to every occupation in America. Together, they made the abstract tangible in a week.

3

NVIDIA projects $1 trillion in orders — and Wall Street shrugs

Economy

At GTC 2026 in San Jose on March 16, NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang told 30,000 attendees from 190 countries that purchase orders for the company’s Blackwell and Vera Rubin chip platforms are on track to reach $1 trillion through 2027 — double the $500 billion projection from a year earlier. CNBC reported that Huang framed the shift as the “inflection point of inference”: the AI economy is transitioning from training models to running them in production, fuelling demand for the Vera Rubin platform, which integrates seven new chips across 1.3 million components per rack and promises 10x performance per watt over its predecessor. AWS will deploy more than one million NVIDIA GPUs starting this year. Microsoft Azure was named the first hyperscale cloud provider to power up Vera Rubin NVL72 systems.

Wall Street was conspicuously unimpressed. NVIDIA’s stock dropped roughly 3.5% during the keynote and ended the day barely positive. TechCrunch reported that the trillion-dollar figure, while doubling prior guidance, also extended the timeline through 2027 — which institutional investors read as slower near-term growth. TD Cowen analysts noted the core problem: “The market cap has gotten so large that Nvidia no longer trades like other stocks … there are trading and fund-flow dynamics at play with a greater-than-$4-trillion company that we, and investors, are not used to.” Fortune observed a separate irony: Huang has somehow avoided the public backlash directed at other AI executives, despite forecasting a larger buildout than any of them. The gap between Silicon Valley’s confidence and Wall Street’s caution mirrors the trust chasm documented in Development 4 — insiders and outsiders looking at the same technology and reaching opposite conclusions.

Why this matters: A trillion-dollar order book is evidence that the capital commitment is now genuinely irreversible — these are multi-year contracts, not speculative positions. But the stock market’s indifference to the biggest product announcement in semiconductor history tells a story the keynote did not: investors are no longer asking whether the infrastructure gets built. They are asking whether anything profitable runs on it.

4

The AI trust chasm: 87% in China, 32% in the United States

Society

Pew Research Center data shows that 50% of Americans say the increasing use of AI makes them more concerned than excited — up from 37% in 2021. Only 10% say they are more excited than concerned. Majorities believe AI will worsen creativity (53%) and the ability to form meaningful relationships (50%). Trust in the country’s ability to regulate AI well is split: 44% have at least some trust, 47% do not. This is the population building the world’s most advanced AI systems — and it is among the least enthusiastic about what they do.

The contrast with other countries is striking. Edelman’s Trust Barometer flash poll found AI trust at 87% in China, 67% in Brazil, 39% in Germany, 36% in the UK, and 32% in the United States. That gap is not a survey artefact — it tracks consistently across polling firms, methodologies, and time periods. In high-trust countries, governments present AI as national strategy and citizens experience adoption as economic opportunity. In the US, the gap between AI’s corporate advocates and public sentiment is widening into a politically exploitable chasm. Brookings analyst Darrell West documented this week how rising electricity bills linked to data centres helped Democrats win governorships in Virginia and New Jersey in 2025. AI distrust is no longer an opinion — it is a voter mobilisation issue heading into the midterms.

Why this matters: The country that leads the world in AI development is among the least trusting of its own technology. That is not just a communications failure — it is a structural vulnerability. Societies that don’t trust their own infrastructure eventually restrict it.

5

The electricity bill arrives

Environment

Brookings researchers Charles Lane and Joseph Kane published a detailed analysis documenting what many Americans have already noticed on their utility bills. Electricity costs have risen 42% since 2019, outpacing the 29% increase in the Consumer Price Index over the same period. Northern Virginia — the world’s largest data centre market, larger than the next five US markets combined — has seen residential disconnections climb in lockstep with data centre load growth since 2022. Dominion Energy Virginia projects data centre demand reaching 13,353 megawatts by 2038, a 3.5x increase from current levels. The projected national power shortfall through 2028: 49 gigawatts.

The political response is accelerating. The Virginia Senate passed a budget bill that would revoke a $1.6 billion tax break for data centre equipment. Households in states with high data centre concentrations have seen rates climb by as much as 16% in a single year. Utilities requested a record $31 billion in rate hikes nationally in 2025. The Trump administration’s Ratepayer Protection Pledge — a voluntary commitment from tech companies to cover their own power costs — is widely regarded as unenforceable. Harvard’s Ari Peskoe noted the pledge targets the wrong entities: cost allocation is determined by utilities and state regulators, not by the companies consuming the power.

Why this matters: The energy cost of the AI buildout is no longer a projection — it is arriving on household utility bills. The political backlash is real, bipartisan, and intensifying. The irony: the same federal government asking Congress to preempt state AI regulation is watching states take the lead on the one AI issue voters actually feel in their wallets.

Sources

  • Brookings Institution, “Confronting and addressing rising energy bills linked to data centers”
what it means

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #12, March 22, 2026

What’s Working

Karpathy’s autoresearch is the clearest demonstration yet that AI agents can conduct the iterative experiment-evaluate-improve cycle that constitutes real scientific work. Seven hundred experiments in two days, with transferable improvements to larger models, on a single GPU. That is not hype — it is a measurable acceleration of the research process that any lab can replicate. The White House framework, whatever its shortcomings, represents the first attempt at a unified federal AI policy position. A patchwork of 1,000+ state bills was genuinely untenable. And NVIDIA’s $1 trillion order book, though it raises serious questions about returns, confirms that the physical infrastructure for the next generation of AI is being built. The commitment is real.

What’s Not

The White House framework proposes federal preemption without federal capacity. Stripping states of regulatory authority while declining to create a federal regulator is not deregulation — it is a regulatory vacuum. The liability protections for AI developers arrive before the technology’s real-world harms have been fully catalogued, let alone adjudicated. NVIDIA’s stock dropped during the biggest product announcement in semiconductor history — a signal that even the investors funding the AI buildout are no longer convinced that trillion-dollar infrastructure commitments will produce trillion-dollar returns on any foreseeable timeline. Meanwhile, 42% of American households are paying materially more for electricity, the Ratepayer Protection Pledge has no enforcement mechanism, and the Harvard analysis identifying the wrong enforcement target has not been addressed. The trust data is equally sobering: 50% of Americans are more concerned than excited about AI, and only 10% are genuinely enthusiastic. The world’s leading AI economy is building on a foundation of public scepticism and investor doubt.

What Nobody’s Saying

The deepest tension this week is between the speed of technical capability and the speed of institutional response — and nobody is honest about the gap. Karpathy’s agents completed 700 experiments in 48 hours. The White House framework will take months to become legislation, if it becomes legislation at all. NVIDIA’s $1 trillion order book locks in infrastructure commitments through 2027; the policy framework governing that infrastructure is still a request to Congress. And the trust chasm — 87% in China, 32% in the US — is not a problem of messaging. It reflects a real difference in how these two societies experience AI: as national strategy with shared benefits, or as corporate extraction with distributed costs. The preemption doctrine assumes the federal government can move faster than states. Every data point this week suggests the opposite.

Bottom line

The infrastructure is being built on the assumption that governance will catch up. It won’t — not at the speed required. The organisations and policymakers positioned best for what comes next are not waiting for the federal framework. They are building institutional capacity now, because the gap between what AI can do and what any government is prepared to manage is widening every week this issue goes to press.
