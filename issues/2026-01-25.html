DeepSeek Changes the Calculus

One year after DeepSeek R1 crashed markets with a $6 million frontier model, the full verdict is in — and it's more complicated than either the panic or the recovery suggest.

3rd Take · Sunday, January 25, 2026

One year ago this week, DeepSeek released R1 — a reasoning model that matched frontier systems at one-tenth the cost. The market panicked: Nvidia lost $600 billion in a single day, every major AI laboratory slashed prices, and the assumptions that organized the industry fractured overnight. Now, twelve months later, the verdict is more nuanced than either the initial terror or the subsequent recovery suggested. The structural effects — cost compression, open-source parity, export control failure — have permanently altered the landscape, even as the market has moved on.

this week
1

One year after DeepSeek R1, the market delivers its verdict

Economy

On January 20, 2025, DeepSeek released R1 — a reasoning model trained for $6 million that matched systems costing $100–500 million. Within a week, Nvidia had shed $600 billion in market capitalisation in a single day — the largest single-day loss in stock market history. Every major AI laboratory slashed prices. The assumption that frontier AI required frontier resources was shattered. One year later, Bloomberg reports that the initial panic was largely a mirage: Nvidia became the first company to reach a $5 trillion valuation in October 2025, Broadcom shares rose 49% across the year, and ASML stock gained 36%.

The Peterson Institute for International Economics (PIIE) analysis confirms the pattern: the DeepSeek shock shook confidence in two assumptions — that US export controls would maintain a durable lead, and that surging AI demand would keep driving chip investment. The first assumption proved wrong. The second held. Hyperscaler capex spending reached approximately $400 billion in 2025, up 70% year-over-year, with consensus estimates for 2026 now exceeding $527 billion. Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet are expected to deploy roughly $475 billion in capital expenditures this year. The "Jevons paradox" played out precisely: cheaper AI made more AI economically viable, which increased total demand for compute rather than reducing it.

But the structural effects of DeepSeek R1 are permanent, even if the market panic was not. The cost equation that governed the AI race for three years collapsed: open-source models now compete at parity with proprietary systems, the set of actors capable of building competitive AI expanded dramatically, and price cuts of 30–50% across the industry became the new baseline. DeepSeek's efficiency innovations — Mixture of Experts architecture, Multi-head Latent Attention — are now standard techniques. What was a competitive moat in early 2025 is a commodity by early 2026.

Why this matters: The market says DeepSeek was a blip; the technology says it was a permanent structural shift. Both are true. The panic was misplaced, but the cost compression it triggered is irreversible — and every strategy built on capital as a moat needs revision.

2

Anthropic launches Claude Cowork — the shift from chat to agency

Society

In the week of January 19, Anthropic expanded Claude Cowork — its autonomous "computer agent" — from research preview to Team and Enterprise plans, signalling a production-grade commitment to the product. Cowork represents a structural departure from the chatbot paradigm that has dominated AI deployment since ChatGPT's release. Rather than responding to user prompts, it observes the user's screen, understands context, and completes repetitive, multi-step work without waiting for instruction. The same week, Anthropic published a new 23,000-word constitution for Claude — the first time a major AI lab formally addressed the possibility that its model may have some form of consciousness or moral status.

The product announcement came alongside news of Anthropic's expansion of Labs — a team focused on "incubating experimental products at the frontier of Claude's capabilities" — with Instagram co-founder Mike Krieger joining as Labs lead. The signal is clear: Anthropic is pivoting toward agency, autonomy, and embedded workflows rather than standalone chat interfaces. The architectural implications matter. A chat system answers questions one at a time. An agent completes tasks end-to-end, which requires different safety frameworks, accountability structures, and governance models. The shift is not just product strategy — it is a governance shift.

Cowork also announced partnerships with GOV.UK (Anthropic selected to build an AI-powered assistant for UK government services) and ServiceNow (Claude becoming the default model in ServiceNow Build Agent). The week of announcements positioned Claude not as a better chatbot, but as the infrastructure for delegated autonomous work. That framing is what matters for understanding how organizations will deploy AI in 2026.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth disguised as a product launch — the shift from chatbot to autonomous agent changes the liability, accountability, and governance framework entirely, and most organisations adopting it have not recognised the shift they are making.

3

Cursor's autonomous agents build production software in days

Work

Cursor, a developer-focused AI IDE, documented its autonomous coding agents building functional applications in dramatically compressed timeframes. Most remarkably, the agents constructed a web browser from scratch in under a week — a project that would normally require months of engineering. The browser rendered Google and other major sites with only minor visual glitches, demonstrating that AI-directed autonomous development is no longer a research topic but an operational reality.

The technical findings matter more than the headline. Cursor reported that GPT-5.2 outperforms Claude for extended autonomous tasks (multi-hour, multi-file projects), likely because of differences in how the models handle long reasoning chains and complex state management. The team ran hundreds of concurrent agents on single projects, documenting the patterns that allow autonomous systems to manage complexity without human intervention. A project that required 1 million lines of code was completed through pure agent execution with minimal human oversight.

The week's announcements coincided with reports from other AI labs on autonomous capabilities. GPTZero found over 100 hallucinated citations in papers accepted at NeurIPS — including fabricated author names and fake DOIs — suggesting that AI systems are approaching the ability to forge research that passes peer review. Researchers at multiple labs provided mathematical proof that large language models have fundamental computational limitations on certain tasks. The simultaneous celebration of unprecedented autonomous capability and the discovery of capacity limitations defines the technical moment: AI systems are becoming genuinely powerful and genuinely limited in specific, measurable ways.

Why this matters: This is progress with a dark twin — autonomous agents can now build production software in days, but the same systems hallucinate citations convincingly enough to pass peer review, and we have no reliable way to tell when they are creating versus fabricating.

US export controls on AI chips no longer work — new strategy needed

Governance

The US has maintained strict export controls on advanced AI chips since 2022 — specifically Nvidia H100 and H200 GPUs — premised on a straightforward security logic: frontier AI requires frontier compute, so restrict compute access and you restrict frontier capability. DeepSeek invalidated that assumption with empirical evidence. An architecture optimized for efficiency can achieve frontier performance with chips well below the export control threshold.

The policy response has been to tighten controls further: adding more chip types and more countries to restricted lists. But tighter controls on an invalid assumption do not increase security — they only impose costs on compliant organizations without achieving strategic effect. If architectural efficiency increasingly substitutes for raw compute, the leverage point for controlling AI capability shifts from hardware to algorithms. That is a much harder thing to control. The US government's technology and security policy communities have not yet fully internalized this implication, which means the next round of export control escalations are likely to be as ineffective as the previous ones.

The strategic consequence is significant. An enforcement regime that fails to achieve its stated goal — while imposing real compliance costs on the organizations subject to it — eventually erodes legitimacy and compliance. The organizations still adhering to export controls in late 2026 will be doing so more from habit and hedging than from genuine conviction that the controls work. That deterioration of norm-based compliance is itself a security problem, though a slower-moving one than frontier AI capability in unfriendly hands.

Why this matters: This is a setback masquerading as policy — the US is tightening controls that have already been proven ineffective, and the real cost is not to adversaries but to the credibility of the entire export control regime.

DeepSeek reveals MODEL1 architecture — V4 is on the horizon

Economy

On January 21 — exactly one year after releasing R1 — DeepSeek quietly updated its FlashMLA codebase on GitHub, revealing a new architecture designated "MODEL1." The identifier appears 28 times across 114 files in the repository, clearly distinguished from DeepSeek-V3.2 (codenamed "V32" internally). Code analysis by developers reveals changes in key-value cache layout, sparsity handling, and FP8 data format decoding, pointing to a fundamental architectural restructuring for memory optimisation and computational efficiency.

The update signals that DeepSeek V4 — the lab's next flagship model — is imminent. Reports indicate V4 is optimised primarily for coding and long-context software engineering tasks, with internal tests suggesting it could outperform Claude and ChatGPT on long-context coding benchmarks. The Financial Times reported that V4 will be multimodal, handling images, video, and text, and that DeepSeek has worked with Chinese chipmakers Huawei and Cambricon to optimise the model for their hardware. The architecture incorporates DeepSeek's recently published "Engram" conditional memory system — a dual-sparsity approach separating factual recall from reasoning — which allows efficient retrieval from contexts exceeding one million tokens.

The strategic implications matter as much as the technical ones. A Reuters report revealed that a senior US administration official confirmed DeepSeek's newest model was trained on Nvidia's top-tier Blackwell chip using a cluster in mainland China — potentially violating US export controls. DeepSeek has also excluded US chipmakers from early V4 testing, granting access instead to domestic suppliers. The pattern is clear: DeepSeek is building to be less dependent on American infrastructure, not more — and achieving it while matching or exceeding frontier capabilities.

Why this matters: DeepSeek's anniversary is not just symbolic — MODEL1 proves the efficiency revolution that began with R1 is accelerating. A new architecture on domestic hardware, independent of American supply chains, is exactly what export controls were designed to prevent.

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #4, January 25, 2026

What's Working

The one-year retrospective on DeepSeek R1 delivers genuinely good news: the Jevons paradox played out exactly as optimists predicted. Cheaper AI made more AI economically viable, which increased total demand for compute rather than reducing it. Hyperscaler capex hit $400 billion in 2025 — up 70% — and is projected to exceed $527 billion in 2026. Nvidia reached $5 trillion. The cost compression that terrified markets in January 2025 turned out to be the best thing that happened to the AI industry: 30–50% API price cuts transferred value directly from AI providers to AI users, expanding the addressable market dramatically. The autonomous development capabilities documented by Cursor — a functional web browser built in under a week, million-line codebases managed by agents — demonstrate that delegation is now operationally real. The question has shifted from "can AI do this?" to "how do we integrate this?" That is progress by any meaningful definition.

What's Not

The US export control regime has been empirically invalidated — and DeepSeek's MODEL1 reveal makes the failure concrete. DeepSeek trained its next-generation model on Nvidia's Blackwell chips in mainland China, potentially violating the very controls designed to prevent exactly this. The policy response has been to tighten restrictions further, adding more chip types and more countries to restricted lists — the classic pattern of doubling down on assumptions that have already failed. If architectural efficiency can substitute for raw compute, restricting chip access no longer restricts frontier capability. Meanwhile, DeepSeek is actively building independence from American infrastructure, working with Huawei and Cambricon on domestic chip optimisation. An enforcement regime that fails to achieve its stated goal while imposing real compliance costs on allied organisations eventually erodes both legitimacy and compliance. The organisations still adhering to export controls in late 2026 will be doing so more from habit and hedging than from genuine conviction that the controls work.

What Nobody's Saying

The shift from chat to agency — which Anthropic is articulating through Cowork but which every major lab will eventually embrace — is a governance shift disguised as a product shift, and nobody in the industry has an incentive to say so clearly. A chatbot answers questions; a human reviews the output. An agent completes work end-to-end. The accountability frameworks are entirely different. When Claude suggests an incorrect argument in a legal brief, the lawyer catches it. When Cowork drafts a contract autonomously, the liability exposure changes fundamentally. The organisations deploying agents in 2026 are accepting a different risk posture than the ones deploying chatbots in 2024 — but most are not consciously making that choice. It is embedded in the product decisions they inherit from their vendors. The same pattern appears in the Cursor story: autonomous agents that can build production software can also hallucinate citations convincingly enough to pass peer review. GPTZero found over 100 fabricated references in NeurIPS-accepted papers. The industry celebrates the building capability without acknowledging that the fabrication capability is the same skill applied differently. The governance question — "what framework should apply when AI systems complete work autonomously?" — does not resolve itself through engineering iteration. It requires deliberate institutional choices that no one is yet making, because making them would slow adoption.

Bottom line

One year after DeepSeek R1, the cost equation that governed the AI race has permanently changed — but not in the way anyone predicted. The market recovered; the structural effects did not reverse. What collapsed was not AI investment but the assumption that capital alone constitutes a moat. The next phase belongs to organisations that deploy AI systematically into specific workflows, not just those that can build the most capable models. DeepSeek's MODEL1 reveal suggests the efficiency revolution is only accelerating.
