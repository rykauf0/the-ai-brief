Work Governance Environment

The Week Everyone Admitted the Problem

At Davos, the people building AI warned about the jobs it will destroy. Meanwhile, export controls faltered, energy demands collided with climate goals, and sovereignty questions went unanswered.

3rd Take · Sunday, January 18, 2026

Davos week produced something unusual: honesty. The people building the most powerful AI systems said, publicly and on the record, that those systems will eliminate a significant share of entry-level white-collar jobs within years. The same week, US export controls were quietly recalibrated after the original approach failed. Energy tensions between AI infrastructure and climate goals intensified. And governments began confronting the sovereignty question they had been avoiding. Four developments, each distinct — but together they describe a week in which the consequences of AI infrastructure became impossible to deny.

this week
1

At Davos, AI leaders warn half of entry-level white-collar jobs could vanish within years

Work

On January 20, at the World Economic Forum in Davos, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei and Google DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis appeared together in a session called "The Day After AGI," moderated by The Economist's editor-in-chief Zanny Minton Beddoes. Amodei stated that AI is "six to 12 months" from doing nearly all functions of a software engineer, and warned that 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs could vanish within one to five years, with unemployment potentially spiking to 10–20%. Hassabis was more measured but agreed that the earliest effects are already emerging at the junior end of organisations — internships and entry-level roles are feeling the impact first.

The workforce warnings extended beyond the AI builders. BlackRock CEO Larry Fink warned that "if AI does to white-collar workers what globalisation did to blue-collar workers, we need to confront that reality directly." IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva described AI as a "tsunami" hitting labour markets, with 40% of jobs "touched by AI." The WEF released a companion report — "Four Futures for Jobs in the New Economy" — modelling scenarios from accelerated progress to stalled adoption. Even the most optimistic scenario projects significant displacement before new roles emerge.

The significance is not the prediction itself — forecasts of AI-driven job loss are common. It is who is making it. When the CEOs of the two leading AI labs publicly warn about mass displacement of the workforce their technology serves, they are conceding something that corporate optimism usually conceals: the people best positioned to understand AI's trajectory believe the labour market impact will be severe, near-term, and concentrated among workers who have no time to retrain.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth delivered by the people who would benefit most from downplaying it — the builders of AI are now publicly warning about mass job displacement, and their timelines are measured in months, not decades.

2

US semiconductor export controls relaxed, but with new strategic constraints

Governance

The US Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security published a final rule effective January 15, 2026, that shifts the licensing policy for advanced computing semiconductors destined for China from presumption of denial to case-by-case review with strict conditions. The rule covers Nvidia's H200 and comparable chips, provided manufacturers meet certification requirements including supply verification and third-party testing. To qualify for export, manufacturers must demonstrate that global semiconductor foundry capacity will not be diverted from US customers, and shipments to China and Macau cannot exceed 50% of total shipments to US customers.

The policy shift signals a recalibration rather than a reversal. The US is no longer attempting a blanket ban on advanced AI chips to China—a policy that proved technically infeasible and diplomatically costly. Instead, it is attempting to manage the rate of technology transfer through conditional licensing. The 50% cap on China shipments and the requirement for supply-chain traceability create friction that raises costs and limits access without a total prohibition. The practical effect is a slower pathway for Chinese AI development, not prevention. DeepSeek's January 2026 release with USD 6 million of development cost, using available chips constrained by export controls, demonstrates that the technical barriers to competitive AI development remain lower than the political constraints imposed by restrictions.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth — the US is quietly admitting that blanket chip bans failed, but the replacement policy of conditional licensing is a half-measure that neither prevents Chinese AI progress nor fully reopens trade.

3

Data centre power demand creates energy security tensions across jurisdictions

Environment

The International Energy Agency projects that global data centre power consumption will double from roughly 500 TWh in 2022 to 1,000 TWh by 2026—consumption equivalent to Japan's entire national electricity usage. AI workloads now account for approximately half of that consumption, having surpassed Bitcoin mining as the fastest-growing source of electricity demand in the sector. Goldman Sachs estimated AI will require an additional 47 gigawatts of US power capacity by 2030. The hyperscaler nuclear energy commitments—Meta's 6.6 GW by 2035, Google and Amazon's comparable deals—indicate that companies are no longer willing to wait for utilities to provide AI-grade power. They are building dedicated supply.

For electricity-constrained regions, particularly in Europe, this creates an allocation question with no obvious answer: should scarce renewable and nuclear capacity be dedicated to AI data centres, or reserved for residential, industrial, and transportation uses? Several European governments have begun asking this question explicitly. Germany's energy ministry has raised concerns about AI data centre power demand affecting industrial competitiveness. France has questioned whether dedicating nuclear capacity to foreign-owned data centres serves national interests. The tension between aggressive AI deployment and current net-zero climate commitments remains unresolved. Organisations simultaneously committed to AI adoption and net-zero targets need specific, costed plans for renewable energy procurement—not general aspirations.

Why this matters: This is a setback for climate commitments — AI companies are reviving nuclear and consuming renewable capacity at a pace that directly competes with decarbonisation goals, and no one has a credible plan to reconcile the two.

4

AI infrastructure sovereignty becomes explicit policy concern across democracies

Governance

Throughout 2025 and into January 2026, major democracies began explicitly grappling with the distinction between AI investment and AI infrastructure sovereignty. Microsoft announced C$7.5 billion in Canadian AI investment. Similar announcements covered the UK, Germany, France, Japan, and Australia. The investments bring capital, jobs, and AI capacity to countries that would otherwise have less. But several European governments have begun asking a governance question: who owns the infrastructure matters. An organisation using a foreign-owned data centre to process citizen data, proprietary research, or security-sensitive information is dependent on the owner's terms of service, pricing decisions, and compliance with its own government's data governance requirements.

The sovereignty question becomes acute when political relationships between countries shift. Several European governments are now developing frameworks for "AI sovereignty" that distinguish between welcoming AI investment and maintaining control over national AI infrastructure. The EU is exploring its own large-scale infrastructure initiatives. The UK is considering domestic data centre requirements for sensitive workloads. The tension between accessing world-class AI infrastructure and maintaining independence over critical data is visible in every government's policy debates. Most countries have not yet made their choice explicit—but the choice is becoming unavoidable as hyperscalers expand foreign presence.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth nobody wants to name — democracies are welcoming foreign AI investment with open arms while quietly realising they are trading sovereignty for capability, and most have not yet admitted the tradeoff to their own citizens.

what it means

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #3, January 18, 2026

What's Working

The Davos workforce warnings are paradoxically positive — not because job displacement is good news, but because the honesty is unprecedented. When the CEOs of the two leading AI labs publicly warn about mass displacement, they are creating the political conditions for a policy response. Previous technology transitions — globalisation, automation, the internet — were characterised by corporate denial until the damage was visible. This time, the builders are warning before the worst effects materialise. The WEF's "Four Futures" report provides a framework for scenario planning that governments and organisations can use immediately. The nuclear revival being driven by AI demand is also genuinely positive: the economic incentive to build clean energy capacity at scale is creating infrastructure that will outlast the current AI cycle.

What's Not

The semiconductor export control story reveals the limits of technological gatekeeping. The US can slow Chinese AI development through restrictions on advanced chip exports, but it cannot prevent it. The shift from blanket denial to conditional licensing is an acknowledgement that the original policy failed, but the replacement is a compromise that satisfies no one: too restrictive to rebuild trade relationships, too permissive to achieve security objectives. Meanwhile, the energy demands of AI infrastructure are colliding with climate commitments across Europe. Germany's energy ministry is raising concerns about industrial competitiveness. France is questioning whether nuclear capacity should serve foreign-owned data centres. No government has yet produced a credible plan that reconciles aggressive AI deployment with existing net-zero targets — they are simply pursuing both and hoping the contradiction resolves itself.

What Nobody's Saying

The most uncomfortable truth from Davos is the gap between what AI leaders said publicly and what their companies are doing. Amodei warned about mass job displacement — while Anthropic aggressively deploys autonomous agents into enterprise workflows. Hassabis counselled undergraduates to become "proficient with AI tools" — while DeepMind builds systems designed to make those tools obsolete. The warnings are sincere. The business strategies are unchanged. That contradiction is not hypocrisy — it is the structural reality of a competitive industry where slowing down unilaterally means losing to someone who doesn't. The sovereignty question deepens the problem: most countries have already made their choice by default, welcoming foreign AI investment without acknowledging what they are conceding — control over infrastructure that will process their citizens' data, their companies' intellectual property, and their security workloads. The global norm-setting process is being driven by those who build, not those who buy. The window for making that investment is narrowing as infrastructure decisions lock in for decades.

Bottom line

The people building AI are now warning about its consequences faster than governments can respond. The honesty is welcome. The absence of any mechanism to translate those warnings into policy — before the displacement arrives — is the defining failure of the current moment.
