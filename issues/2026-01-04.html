Issue #1 · Sunday, January 4, 2026

Governance Economy Workforce

The Year That Got Serious

How 2026 became the year AI moved from experiment to infrastructure — and why governance will define the decade

The AI Brief · Sunday, January 4, 2026

The story of AI in 2025 was one of possibility made tangible. Large language models matured from curiosities to tools. The question was exploratory: can this work? 2026 is different. The new question — harder, more urgent, more revealing of institutional competence — is how organisations will embed AI into critical operations. Not as a parallel experiment. As infrastructure.

this week
1

2026 begins with a regulatory collision over AI governance

Governance & Policy

As 2026 opened, new state-level AI regulations took effect just as the Trump administration moved to challenge them. Colorado's comprehensive AI Act became enforceable on January 1. California's requirements for generative AI developers to publish training data summaries (AB 2013) also took effect. Simultaneously, President Trump issued an executive order—Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence—that directs federal agencies to identify and challenge state laws deemed inconsistent with federal AI policy. The Commerce Department has until March 11, 2026 to evaluate state AI laws and refer problematic ones to a new AI Litigation Task Force.

The collision is immediate and material. Organisations operating across multiple states face a governance environment that is actively fragmenting rather than consolidating. California and Colorado have built comprehensive frameworks; the federal government has now explicitly signalled it will challenge them; other states are still developing their own rules. For any organisation with genuinely sensitive data or high-stakes AI applications, the safest posture is to build for the most stringent possible requirements—but that strategy only works if you know which requirements will survive the legal challenge. That clarity will not come until mid-2026 at the earliest.

2

The agentic AI market reaches critical adoption threshold

Economy & Markets

Gartner released projections at the start of 2026 showing that 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by the end of this year—a significant jump from the 5–10% adoption rates of late 2025. The global agentic AI market is now valued at USD 9.14 billion and is projected to reach USD 139.19 billion by 2034, with a compound annual growth rate of 40.5%. More than 1,041 active companies now operate in the agentic AI sector, with USD 13.8 billion in total funding concentrated primarily in North America.

The infrastructure and talent implications are substantial. An agent that books a flight, files an expense report, and schedules a follow-up meeting creates operational value that a chatbot cannot match. But agents also create accountability complexity: when the system makes a decision autonomously, the human is no longer in the loop before consequences materialise. Organisations deploying agents at scale in 2026 are making real-time accountability decisions without the governance frameworks that should precede such decisions. The gap between capability deployment and governance readiness is widening precisely at the moment when financial services, healthcare, and legal organisations are moving agents into production.

3

DeepSeek releases frontier-class reasoning model, challenging cost assumptions

Economy & Markets

On January 20, 2025, Chinese AI company DeepSeek released DeepSeek-R1, an open-source reasoning model with 671 billion parameters that rivals GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet across major benchmarks. The model was developed in two months for less than USD 6 million—a fraction of the USD 100+ million cost that frontier US labs typically invest in comparable systems. The API is priced 95% cheaper than OpenAI's equivalent offering. By January 27, DeepSeek-R1 surpassed ChatGPT as the most-downloaded free app on the iOS App Store in the United States, triggering an 18% drop in Nvidia's share price.

The economic and strategic implications are immediate. The assumption that frontier AI development requires proportional capital investment is no longer reliable. A well-resourced team with focused engineering and access to open-source model architectures can produce genuinely competitive systems at far lower cost. For organisations assessing the capital required to develop competitive in-house AI capability, the DeepSeek model is a useful recalibration: the barrier is lower than previously assumed, but it still requires genuine technical depth. The geopolitical dimension is also significant: the US semiconductor export controls intended to limit Chinese AI development did not prevent DeepSeek from building a frontier-class system with available chips.

The nuclear infrastructure race for AI becomes explicit

Economy & Infrastructure

Meta announced on January 9 a series of contracts with nuclear energy companies—Vistra, Oklo, and TerraPower—to secure up to 6.6 gigawatts of nuclear power by 2035 to supply its AI data centre operations. The agreement with Vistra alone will provide more than 2.1 gigawatts from operating and newly expanded nuclear plants in Ohio and Pennsylvania. The deals position Meta as one of the largest corporate purchasers of nuclear energy in American history. Similar announcements from Google, Amazon, and Microsoft throughout 2025 signalled the same trend: hyperscalers are no longer waiting for utilities to provide AI-grade power. They are signing direct contracts to build or expand nuclear facilities.

This represents a structural shift in how energy infrastructure is deployed. AI data centre power demand has become so large and so predictable that companies are willing to commit billions of dollars and multi-year timelines to dedicated power sources. The nuclear revival being driven by AI demand is one of the more consequential and least anticipated consequences of the AI infrastructure buildout. For electricity-constrained regions, this creates a new allocation question: should scarce nuclear capacity be dedicated to AI data centres, or reserved for residential and industrial uses? Several European governments are beginning to ask that question explicitly.

what it means

What It All Means

The AI Brief · Issue #1, January 4, 2026

Four developments from the opening week of 2026, each distinct, each rooted in a separate primary source. But together they describe a single transition: the moment when AI infrastructure became the infrastructure—when the question shifted from 'can we build this?' to 'who governs it, and on whose terms?'

The governance collision is the most structurally significant story in the medium term. The US is simultaneously in the position of having no federal AI legislation while the executive branch actively challenges state laws that attempt to fill the vacuum. The EU, by contrast, has built a coherent (if demanding) framework through the AI Act. For organisations operating internationally, this creates a two-tier world: regions with clear rules (EU) and regions with governance uncertainty (US). The safe strategy is also the expensive strategy: build for the highest standard possible and hope that standard survives the political challenge.

The agentic AI adoption curve is moving faster than the governance readiness curve. That's not a new problem—it's the defining technology adoption problem of the last two decades. But it matters more for agents than for chatbots, because agents make decisions that propagate. A recommendation engine can be evaluated in retrospect; an agent that approves a medical procedure or a financial transaction or a hiring decision has already acted. The governance frameworks need to be in place before the agent is deployed, not after the first error.

The DeepSeek result is economically significant for one clear reason: it proves that the capital required to build frontier AI systems is lower and more accessible than US labs had claimed. That doesn't mean every company can do it—technical depth is still required, and access to sophisticated open-source models matters enormously. But it does mean the playing field is less tilted toward incumbents than previously assumed. For strategists assessing whether to build or buy AI capability, the DeepSeek model is evidence that building is more viable than the venture capital narrative had suggested.

The nuclear infrastructure story connects to the broader pattern: the energy required to operate AI at scale is so large, and so certain, that companies are making multi-billion-dollar commitments to dedicated power sources. This is not speculative infrastructure investment. This is companies acting on the conviction that AI data centre power demand will continue to grow. If they're wrong, those are stranded assets. If they're right, the energy constraint becomes the real bottleneck in AI deployment. Either way, the decision to commit billions to nuclear development is a statement that AI infrastructure is foundational infrastructure, not a temporary workload.

The coherent signal across all four developments is this: 2026 is the year that AI moved from the technology category to the infrastructure category. Infrastructure gets governed. It gets regulated. It gets subjected to long-term capital allocation decisions. It becomes politically consequential. Every organisation that treats 2026 as the year to move deliberately—to ask the governance questions, to understand the regulatory landscape, to make deliberate infrastructure choices—will be setting the terms. The ones that treat it as the year to monitor will find the terms set for them.

Bottom line

The transition from AI-as-experiment to AI-as-infrastructure is the defining governance challenge of the decade. The organisations and governments that address the governance questions first will have more control over how AI gets embedded. The others will be adapting to terms they didn't help define.
