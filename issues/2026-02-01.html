Issue #5 · Sunday, February 1, 2026

Governance Work

AI Safety Governance Takes Shape

The most comprehensive safety research ever assembled meets the first binding AI regulation. The question is whether governance can keep pace with development.

3rd Take · Sunday, February 1, 2026

The final week of January produced one of the most significant documents on AI risk to date — Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei's 20,000-word essay mapping five categories of existential risk and proposing specific defences. The same week, the EU AI Act moved into active enforcement. And against this backdrop, the European Commission missed its own deadline for publishing compliance guidance, exposing the structural gap between the pace of risk assessment and the pace of actual governance development. Two steps forward, one step back.

this week
1

Anthropic's CEO maps five categories of AI risk — and a defence plan

Governance

On January 26, Dario Amodei — CEO of Anthropic — published "The Adolescence of Technology," a 20,000-word essay arguing that AI poses unprecedented risks to national security, democracy, and the global economy. The essay identifies five distinct categories of risk: autonomous misalignment, biological and chemical weapons misuse, authoritarian consolidation of power, massive economic disruption, and erosion of shared values. Amodei characterises the current moment as a "technological adolescence" — AI is powerful enough to cause serious harm but not mature enough to be fully controlled.

What distinguishes the essay from typical executive AI commentary is its specificity. Amodei proposes concrete defence mechanisms: interpretability research to understand what AI systems are actually doing inside their networks, staged release protocols, constitutional AI training, and specific policy interventions. He argues that 2026 is the critical year for building defences because capability development will accelerate faster than governance can adapt. The essay generated 5.8 million views on social media and extensive coverage across Tier 1 media.

Critics noted the inherent tension: Anthropic's CEO defining what AI risks look like and what defences are needed is also, unavoidably, positioning Anthropic's existing products and approach as the solution. As Fortune observed, "what he thinks is needed to secure humanity's future also aligns well with Anthropic's existing brand positioning." The analysis is genuine and the concern is sincere — but it is also strategic. The two things are not mutually exclusive. The governance implication is significant: when the CEO of a frontier AI lab publicly acknowledges that the technology his company builds poses genuine risks to civilisation, policymakers have an evidence-based starting point that goes beyond abstract speculation.

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth from inside the industry — a leading AI CEO is publicly mapping how his own technology could destabilise nations, while simultaneously arguing that his company's approach is the right defence. The conflict of interest is real, but so is the risk assessment.

2

EU AI Act enforcement begins — the first binding AI regulation with teeth

Governance

The EU AI Act entered active enforcement phase in February 2026 after phased implementation through 2024–2025. The enforcement now covers binding prohibitions: social credit systems by governments, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces without legal authorization, AI that manipulates subconscious behaviour, and systems that exploit psychological vulnerabilities. Non-compliance carries material penalties: up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover — whichever is larger. For a company with €500 million in revenue, that is a €35 million fine. For a company with €500 billion in revenue, the penalty is €35 billion.

The regulation has teeth because it has enforcement. The European Commission began investigations into multiple organizations in early 2026. Several major US technology companies modified their AI products specifically to comply with EU requirements — removing features that worked in North America but violated EU prohibitions, altering transparency mechanisms, adding human oversight steps. This is the clearest possible evidence that binding regulation works. Companies do not change profitable product features because of voluntary frameworks. They change them because legal liability makes non-compliance more expensive than compliance.

The transparency and accountability provisions that take effect August 2, 2026, will be more demanding than most organizations realize. AI used for hiring, credit decisions, education assessment, and law enforcement requires documented impact assessments, human oversight protocols, and mechanisms for appeal. Organizations that have not assessed what their AI systems do in these domains relative to the AI Act requirements are already behind. The compliance clock is no longer abstract.

Why this matters: This is progress with teeth — companies are already changing products to comply, proving that binding regulation with real penalties works where voluntary commitments did not.

3

Anthropic and OpenAI race to agentic coding — with significant restrictions

Work

In the first week of February, Anthropic and OpenAI both announced major agentic coding models — essentially autonomous systems capable of managing complex software development tasks without human direction. Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.6 arrived with a 1 million-token context window and explicitly designed capabilities for "multi-agent collaboration," enabling parallel workflows across tools and coding. OpenAI's GPT-5.3-Codex followed days later, described as the first model involved in creating itself — the Codex team used early versions to debug its own training, manage deployments, and diagnose evaluations.

Both announcements came with unusual caution. OpenAI delayed full developer access to GPT-5.3-Codex and restricted it from high-risk cybersecurity use cases, citing concerns about "unprecedented cybersecurity risks." The decision to hold back access to one's newest model — at a moment when competitors are pushing capabilities forward — is extraordinary. It indicates that the internal threat assessment found sufficiently serious risks that limiting adoption was the preferred response to releasing at full scale. Anthropic positioned Claude 4.6's agent capabilities more aggressively, but with explicit emphasis on accountability framework design.

The contrast reflects different organizational postures toward the governance problem. OpenAI's response is caution and staged release — let the risks emerge in controlled settings before full deployment. Anthropic's response is emphasis on governance — assuming deployment will happen at scale and focusing on how to make it responsible. Neither approach eliminates the fundamental question: when an AI system can autonomously write code, manage deployments, and operate across multiple systems, who is responsible when it makes a mistake that causes real harm?

Why this matters: This is an uncomfortable truth — both labs are shipping autonomous coding agents while openly admitting they cannot fully characterize the risks, and nobody has answered who is liable when an AI agent causes real harm.

4

The EU AI Act compliance deadline slides — mapping governance gaps

Governance

The European Commission missed its February 2 deadline to publish binding guidance on how to determine whether an AI system qualifies as "high-risk" under the AI Act's Article 6. That category encompasses AI used in employment, credit decisions, healthcare, and law enforcement. The guidance was supposed to clarify what compliance looks like; without it, organizations cannot definitively assess whether their systems require the additional scrutiny and oversight Article 6 demands. The August 2, 2026 deadline for Article 6 compliance enforcement is now approaching without the foundational guidance in place.

The Commission's stated reason was reasonable: they needed months to integrate feedback from stakeholder consultations. But the structural problem is not feedback integration — it is fundamental misalignment between the speed of AI capability development and the pace of governance infrastructure. The International AI Safety Report took a year to publish. The EU AI Act took four years to negotiate and draft. Both are moving at governance timescales. AI capability iterations happen in weeks. The gap widens, not narrows.

The enforcement infrastructure has additional constraints. Member state authorities struggled to appoint enforcement officials in 2025. Some jurisdictions lack the technical expertise to evaluate complex AI systems. The standardization bodies tasked with developing technical standards missed their 2025 deadline and are now targeting end-of-2026. None of this indicates failure — it indicates the genuine difficulty of building governance infrastructure for a moving target. But it also indicates that organizations cannot wait for perfect guidance. The ones best positioned for August 2 are the ones that have already assessed what their systems do in Article 6 contexts and are building compliance now, assuming the guidance will be demanding.

Why this matters: This is a setback that reveals a structural problem — even the world's most sophisticated regulatory body cannot write rules fast enough to keep pace with the technology it is trying to govern.

what it means

What It All Means

3rd Take · Issue #5, February 1, 2026

What's Working

Amodei's essay is a genuine contribution to the risk conversation. A sitting CEO of a frontier AI lab publicly mapping five categories of existential risk — with specific, concrete defence proposals — gives policymakers something they have lacked: an evidence-based risk framework from someone who actually builds the technology. The essay's proposals — interpretability research, staged release protocols, constitutional training — are not theoretical. They are documented practices with measurable effects on deployment safety. The governance implication is that the technical capacity to manage risk is increasing, even if the political will to mandate it is not.

The EU AI Act is equally significant. It is binding, it is enforceable, and it is already working. Multiple US technology companies modified their AI products specifically to comply with EU requirements — removing features, altering transparency mechanisms, adding human oversight steps. Companies do not change profitable product features because of voluntary frameworks. They change them because legal liability makes non-compliance more expensive than compliance. That is real governance, and it exists nowhere else in the world at this scale.

What's Not

The EU's own implementation is falling behind. The European Commission missed its February 2 deadline to publish guidance on what qualifies as "high-risk" under Article 6 — the foundational classification that determines which AI systems require additional scrutiny. The August 2026 enforcement deadline is approaching without that guidance in place. Member state authorities struggled to appoint enforcement officials throughout 2025. Standardization bodies missed their deadline for technical standards and are now targeting end-of-2026. The world's most sophisticated regulatory body, backed by a strong economy and deep technical capacity, cannot keep pace with the technology it is regulating. If the EU cannot do it, no one can — and that is a problem for everyone who assumed regulation would catch up.

Meanwhile, the agentic turn arrived before governance was ready. Both Anthropic and OpenAI launched autonomous coding agents in the same week. OpenAI restricted access to GPT-5.3-Codex over cybersecurity concerns serious enough to override competitive pressure. That is a company telling you, through its actions, that the risks are real and present. But restricting one model's access is a stopgap, not a governance framework. The liability question — who is responsible when an autonomous AI agent causes real harm — remains unanswered by any legal system on earth.

What Nobody's Saying

The structural asymmetry between governance and capability development is not a temporary gap that will close with more effort. It is a permanent condition. Safety research publishes on timescales of months. Regulation develops on timescales of years. AI capability iterations happen on timescales of weeks. No amount of institutional improvement changes the fundamental mismatch. Amodei's essay took months to write. The EU AI Act took four years to negotiate and draft. The agentic coding systems that now need governing were conceived, built, and shipped in that same period. The assumption underlying all current governance — that you can discover and address problems before they cause harm — is becoming harder to defend with each capability acceleration.

The deeper uncomfortable truth is that the frontier labs are already functioning as de facto regulators. When OpenAI decides which capabilities to restrict and Anthropic decides which accountability frameworks to require, they are making governance choices that affect millions of users — choices that no elected body reviewed or approved. The labs are not waiting for the EU to clarify what governance looks like. They are building it themselves, on their own terms, with their own incentives. That may be necessary given the pace mismatch. But it means the organizations with the strongest commercial incentive to deploy are also the ones deciding what deployment safeguards look like. That is not a system designed to protect the public interest.

Bottom line

For the first time, a frontier AI CEO has publicly mapped the risks his own technology poses. For the first time, there is binding regulation with teeth. And for the first time, it is clear that both cannot keep pace with the rate of capability development. Organizations need to build their own governance infrastructure now, before mandates force the issue.
